How We Got Here

A referendum in 1971 decided most of this.

In 1965, the Georgia General Assembly created MARTA to serve five core metro counties: Fulton, DeKalb, Cobb, Clayton, and Gwinnett. By the time the funding referendum was put to voters in 1971, only Fulton, DeKalb, and the City of Atlanta approved it. Cobb and Gwinnett opted out. Clayton would not join until 2014.

Historians, journalists, and the Kinder Institute for Urban Research at Rice University have written extensively about why. The 1965 and 1971 votes were not really about transportation. They were about race. Suburban voters were told, and came to believe, that rapid transit would integrate predominantly white neighborhoods, lower home values, and expose their communities to federal busing programs. One state representative at the time described the proposed 15 cent fare as "a gift to a certain segment of the population."

The result is the network Atlanta has today. Rail serves two counties in a metro area of roughly six million. The southwest corridor, where the Georgia State University Law Review identified the most severe transit deficits, is still the hardest to serve. This is the system we inherited, and it is the system the recommendations below are trying to reshape.

Key reading: Kinder Institute, GSU Law Review, Atlanta Magazine.

What's Changed in 2026

More moved in the last month than in the last ten years.

In March 2026, MARTA rolled out three initiatives at once. The service map you grew up with is not the one Atlanta has today.

  • March 7, 2026

    MARTA Reach goes system-wide

    On-demand vans that connect riders to the nearest rail or bus hub. After a 2022 pilot in three zones, Reach relaunched across twelve zones including West Atlanta, East Point, Kirkwood/Candler Park, and Belvedere Park. Rides are free through May 2, then $2.50. The app, website, and call line (404-848-6622) all work. No smartphone or credit card required.

  • March 2026

    NextGen Bus Network goes live

    A ground-up redesign of MARTA's bus routes, running in parallel with Reach. This is the first major bus overhaul in decades and shifts service frequency toward corridors where ridership actually concentrates.

  • April 16, 2026

    Rapid A-Line bus rapid transit opens

    The first true BRT route in Atlanta. Dedicated lanes, signal priority, level boarding. It is one piece of a broader shift from building new rail (slow, expensive) to delivering rail-like service on buses (faster, cheaper, more flexible).

The combined effect is that the southwest corridor and other historically underserved areas have more usable transit today than they did six months ago. Expanding what is already working, rather than inventing something new, is the core of the Scale MARTA Reach recommendation on the poster.

Watch & Read

Hear it from the people who built it.

The MARTA Reach model was not invented by MARTA alone. Georgia Tech's Socially Aware Mobility Lab, under Professor Pascal Van Hentenryck in the School of Industrial and Systems Engineering, designed the routing algorithms and ran the 2022 pilot. The lab has a short explainer video and the pilot outcome reports on its site.

Take Action

Five specific things you can do this week.

Concrete beats vague. If you are reading this as a Georgia Tech student, any one of these moves the needle more than a share on social media.

  1. Volunteer with Propel ATL

    The advocacy group behind the $120M city bond for pedestrian and bike safety upgrades. They run Walk ATL audits, Community Advocates Network meetings, and the Buford Highway community walk audit.

    Sign up →

  2. Take a MARTA Reach trip

    Service is free through May 2. Download the app, call 404-848-6622, or book at book.martareach.rideco.com. Using it generates ridership data that justifies expansion into more zones.

    See coverage map →

  3. Attend a Beltline Transit Study meeting

    Public planning meetings shape which corridors get future transit investment. Showing up is how underserved neighborhoods get back on the map.

    See schedule →

  4. Connect with the SAM Lab

    If you are in CS, ISyE, CEE, or Public Policy, the Socially Aware Mobility Lab takes undergrad researchers. This is the lab that actually built Reach.

    Contact the lab →

  5. Share this page with one person in Cobb or Gwinnett

    The counties that voted MARTA down in 1971 still have the most leverage over expansion. Opinion shifts start in conversation.

    Send a link →
